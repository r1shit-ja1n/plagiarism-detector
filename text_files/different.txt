Assignment: Reading Comprehension and Analysis - "The Great Gatsby"

Q1: Discuss the symbolism of the green light at the end of Daisy's dock.
The green light represents Gatsby's unyielding, almost delusional hope for the future and his desperate desire to reclaim the past with Daisy. On a broader scale, it serves as a powerful symbol for the American Dream—a seemingly attainable but ultimately elusive goal that constantly recedes as one reaches for it.

Q2: What does the Valley of Ashes represent in the novel?
The Valley of Ashes is a bleak, desolate industrial wasteland situated between West Egg and New York City. It symbolizes the moral and social decay that results from the uninhibited pursuit of wealth, as well as the plight of the poor, like George Wilson, who are trapped in the grim reality of the lower class while the rich carelessly indulge themselves.

Q3: Analyze the significance of the eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg.
The faded, bespectacled eyes painted on an old advertising billboard act as a haunting observer over the Valley of Ashes. For characters like Wilson, they represent the eyes of God, silently watching and judging the moral corruption and spiritual emptiness of the Jazz Age, though ultimately, they are just an abandoned advertisement with no real divine power.

Q4: How do Gatsby’s lavish parties reflect the culture of the 1920s?
Gatsby's extravagant weekend parties highlight the superficiality, excess, and sheer decadence of the Roaring Twenties. Guests attend uninvited, consume massive amounts of bootleg liquor, and behave recklessly, entirely oblivious to the host. Gatsby throws these magnificent spectacles not for his own enjoyment, but solely as an elaborate trap in the faint hope that Daisy might one day wander in.

Q5: What is the function of Nick Carraway as the narrator?
Nick serves as both an observer and a participant, offering a grounded perspective amidst the chaotic lives of the incredibly wealthy. By positioning himself as highly tolerant and inclined to reserve judgment, he provides a trustworthy lens for the reader, even though his deep fascination with Gatsby ultimately clouds his supposed objectivity by the end of the narrative.
